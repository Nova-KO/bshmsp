Cybersecurity Best Practices for Nonprofits

Protect your nonprofit's sensitive data and donor information with these essential cybersecurity practices that won't break your budget.

By Anjali • Published January 18, 2025 • 7 min read

Why Nonprofits Are Prime Targets for Cyberattacks

Cybercriminals increasingly target nonprofit organizations because they often have valuable data but limited cybersecurity resources. According to recent studies, nonprofits experience cyberattacks at rates comparable to for-profit organizations, yet many lack the security infrastructure to defend themselves effectively.

Nonprofits typically store sensitive information including:

  • Donor personal and financial information
  • Client and beneficiary data
  • Employee records and payroll information
  • Financial records and grant information
  • Strategic planning documents

Essential Cybersecurity Practices Every Nonprofit Should Implement

1. Strong Password Policies and Multi-Factor Authentication

Weak passwords are the leading cause of data breaches. Implement these password security measures:

  • Require passwords to be at least 12 characters long
  • Use a combination of letters, numbers, and special characters
  • Implement multi-factor authentication (MFA) for all accounts
  • Use password managers to generate and store secure passwords
  • Regularly update passwords, especially for shared accounts

2. Regular Software Updates and Patch Management

Outdated software contains vulnerabilities that hackers exploit. Establish a routine for:

  • Automatic updates for operating systems and security software
  • Regular updates for all applications and plugins
  • Immediate installation of critical security patches
  • Maintaining an inventory of all software and systems

3. Employee Training and Awareness

Your staff are your first line of defense against cyber threats. Provide regular training on:

  • Recognizing phishing emails and social engineering attempts
  • Safe browsing practices and avoiding malicious websites
  • Proper handling of sensitive data
  • Incident reporting procedures
  • Mobile device security for remote work

4. Data Backup and Recovery Plans

Regular backups protect against ransomware and data loss. Implement the 3-2-1 backup rule:

  • 3 copies of important data
  • 2 different storage types (e.g., local and cloud)
  • 1 offsite backup for disaster recovery

Test your backup systems regularly to ensure they work when needed.

5. Network Security Measures

Secure your network infrastructure with:

  • Firewalls to monitor and control network traffic
  • Secure Wi-Fi networks with WPA3 encryption
  • Network segmentation to limit access to sensitive data
  • Regular network monitoring for unusual activity
  • VPN access for remote workers

Budget-Friendly Security Solutions for Nonprofits

Free and Low-Cost Security Tools

Many effective cybersecurity tools are available at no cost or reduced rates for nonprofits:

  • Google Workspace for Nonprofits - Free email and collaboration tools with built-in security
  • Microsoft 365 Nonprofit - Discounted office suite with advanced security features
  • TechSoup - Discounted software and hardware for qualifying nonprofits
  • Free antivirus solutions - Many reputable providers offer free versions

Nonprofit-Specific Security Grants

Several organizations offer cybersecurity grants specifically for nonprofits:

  • The Nonprofit Technology Network (NTEN) security grants
  • Local community foundation technology grants
  • Federal and state cybersecurity assistance programs

Creating a Cybersecurity Incident Response Plan

Even with strong preventive measures, incidents can still occur. Prepare your organization with:

Before an Incident

  • Identify critical systems and data
  • Establish an incident response team
  • Create contact lists for IT support, legal counsel, and law enforcement
  • Document your response procedures

During an Incident

  • Immediately isolate affected systems
  • Document the incident and preserve evidence
  • Notify stakeholders according to your plan
  • Work with cybersecurity professionals to assess the damage

After an Incident

  • Conduct a thorough post-incident review
  • Update security measures based on lessons learned
  • Communicate transparently with donors and stakeholders
  • Review and update your incident response plan
